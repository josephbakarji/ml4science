Defining Your Research Question

ML for Science and Engineering

From data to paper: scope, methods, iteration, and writing

What Is the Project?

A research paper where you ask a scientific question, collect or generate data, apply machine learning methods with scientific inductive biases, iterate on your approach, and write up the results at conference quality.

  • Teams of 2 students
  • 40% of your course grade
  • Final deliverable: 6–9 page paper + oral presentation
  • The journey matters as much as the destination

The goal is not to reproduce someone else's result. It is to create something new, however small the contribution.

Project Scope

Your project must sit at the intersection of two things:

Scientific Modeling

  • A system governed by physical/biological/engineering principles
  • Inductive biases: conservation laws, symmetries, spatio-temporal structure
  • You must use these biases, not ignore them

Machine Learning

  • Data-driven methods from this course or beyond
  • Proper handling of training/test data
  • Hyperparameter tuning, model comparison

What distinguishes this from a generic ML project: the ML model must be informed by the science. Including an interaction term because predators eat prey is a physics-informed decision. That is what we are looking for.

Scientific Inductive Biases

The science determines what constraints you bring to the model. You are not just fitting a black box.

DomainExample Inductive BiasHow It Enters the Model
Ecology Species interact multiplicatively Include xy terms in SINDy library
Fluid dynamics Conservation of mass/momentum Constrain PDE structure, divergence-free fields
Robotics Newtonian mechanics (F = ma) Lagrangian or Hamiltonian neural network
Epidemiology Compartmental structure (SIR) Constrain ODE topology to known compartments
Climate Energy balance, radiative forcing Physics-informed loss terms in PINN

Think about your system: what do you know that should constrain what you learn?

Choosing Your Data

Synthetic Data

  • Simulate a known model, add noise
  • Contribution is methodological: you improve or compare algorithms
  • Good for: benchmarking, ablation studies, controlled experiments
  • You know the ground truth

Real Data

  • Lab measurements, sensor data, field observations, public datasets
  • Contribution is applied: you discover something about the system
  • Messier, harder, more rewarding
  • You learn about the domain, not just the algorithm

Real data is preferred and will earn more points. But synthetic data with a genuine methodological contribution is also valuable.

Where to Find Real Data

  • Your own lab — if you are in a research group, use your own measurements
  • Public repositories — UCI ML Repository, Kaggle, PhysioNet, NOAA, NASA Earthdata
  • Paper reproductions — many papers share their data; start from a paper you find interesting
  • Simulation + real hybrid — simulate a model, validate against real measurements

Temporal, spatial, or spatio-temporal data is ideal for this course. Think: time series, video, sensor arrays, climate records, biological trajectories.

Reading Papers

The single most important skill you will develop in this course.

There are no courses on how to read papers. You learn by doing it. The more you read, the better your sense of what matters, what is interesting, and where your work fits.

How to Read a Paper (Shallow Pass)

1
Read the abstract and introduction carefully
2
Skim the method: what are the inputs, outputs, assumptions?
3
Look at the figures and tables: what story are they telling?
4
Read the conclusion: what did they find? What remains open?

You do not need to understand every equation on the first pass. Get the gist. Understand what question they asked and how they answered it.

Where to Start

  • The SINDy paper (Brunton et al., 2016) and its citation graph
  • The PDE-FIND paper (Rudy et al., 2017)
  • The PINNs paper (Raissi et al., 2019)
  • Follow citations forward: who built on these methods? What is the latest?
  • Use Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar, or Connected Papers

You can use an LLM to help navigate the literature. It is your new search engine. But you still need to read the papers themselves. The LLM summary is not the paper.

Iteration: The Core Requirement

What I evaluate is not whether you found the right answer.
It is how you got there.

The iterative process, which we can call circular thinking:

You are not expected to converge on the first try. You are expected to try, fail, learn, and try again.

Document Everything

Every time you sit down to work on the project, write down what you did.

The Iteration Log (Appendix)

  • What you tried and what hyperparameters you set
  • What worked and what failed
  • What decision you made and why
  • If you consulted an LLM: what you asked, what it suggested, whether you followed it
  • What you plan to try next

This may be 10–20 pages. It is your research notebook. It goes in the appendix of your paper.

Knowing what failed is sometimes more important than knowing what worked. Documenting failures saves others (and your future self) from repeating the same mistakes.

Separating Your Decisions from the LLM's

I need to be able to tell what you decided vs. what the tool decided.

Good

"Results didn't extrapolate well. I hypothesized that multiple initial conditions would give a distribution of solutions. Tested with 10 ICs. Confirmed: ensemble reduces variance by 40%."

Bad

"Asked ChatGPT what to do. It said try more initial conditions. Did that. Got better results."

You can use the LLM to brainstorm. But the junction where you stop, think, and choose a direction: that is where the learning happens. Make those junctions visible.

The standard: could this be submitted to a workshop or conference? It does not need to be perfect. But it should be genuine.

Writing Quality

If you're writing something, you need to captivate me. I need to look at it, and I need to enjoy it.

Go read papers at top venues: NeurIPS, ICLR, Nature, Science, PNAS, Journal of Computational Physics. See how they write. Clear, precise, to the point.

The Raised Baseline

Ten years ago, a good paper from a course like this was impressive. Now you have tools that help you write, code, and find references. The baseline has shifted. A merely competent paper is now the minimum. What impresses is depth, originality, and the evidence that you struggled with the problem and won.

Using LLMs for Writing

Use LLMs To:

  • Edit and refine your prose
  • Check grammar and structure
  • Brainstorm outlines
  • Debug code
  • Find related references

Do Not:

  • Let it write your paper
  • Submit text you haven't read word by word
  • Include sentences you cannot explain
  • Use it to avoid thinking

The paper should read as your voice, informed by your thinking. If a sentence sounds impressive but you cannot explain what it means, delete it.

What Gets a Good Grade

A

  • Clear question, well-motivated
  • Real data or meaningful synthetic benchmark
  • Scientific inductive biases actively used
  • Thorough iteration log showing real struggle
  • Well-written, captivating paper
  • Surprise me with an insight

D/F

  • Vague question, no motivation
  • Bare minimum effort, obvious shortcuts
  • No iteration documented
  • LLM-generated slop: fluent text, confused content
  • No evidence of understanding the method
  • Cannot defend the work orally

There is no middle ground. Mediocre work in an age of powerful tools is a choice, and it shows.

Why This Format?

Learning is when you try to do something new of your own. You come up with an idea. You craft it. You fail. Then you realize there is a better way.

I am not assigning you a problem to solve. I am asking you to find a problem.

  • The act of choosing teaches you about problem selection
  • The act of failing teaches you about methods
  • The act of iterating teaches you about research
  • The act of writing teaches you about clarity of thought

A student who chooses a project, discovers it doesn't work, and pivots has learned more than a student who executes a prescribed plan perfectly.

The Opportunity

We are at a junction.

  • You have access to tools that let you prototype ideas in hours, not weeks
  • You can read, search, and navigate literature faster than any previous generation
  • You can write, debug, and iterate with an AI collaborator at your side

The barrier to trying has almost disappeared.
The barrier to excellence has not.

You can, today, go home and create something new that no one has created before. The question is: will you?

Project Ideas (Starting Points)

These are suggestions, not prescriptions. The best projects come from your own questions.

Equation Discovery

  • SINDy on real ecological or epidemiological data
  • PDE-FIND on fluid flow measurements
  • Symbolic regression for empirical scaling laws
  • Compare SINDy vs. symbolic regression on same system

Physics-Informed ML

  • PINN for inverse problems (parameter estimation)
  • Neural ODE with conservation constraints
  • Hamiltonian/Lagrangian neural networks for mechanical systems
  • Surrogate modeling for expensive simulations

Data-Driven Dynamics

  • DMD/Koopman analysis of video data
  • Infer equations from pendulum/double pendulum video
  • Weather or climate time series forecasting
  • Sensor data from robotics or biomechanics
