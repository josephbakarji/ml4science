Part 1: The Birth of Mathematical Physics

When humans discovered that nature speaks mathematics

Galileo Galilei (1564–1642)

Portrait by Justus Sustermans, 1636

  • Rolled balls down inclined planes
  • Timed with water clocks
  • Discovered: $d \propto t^2$
$$d = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$

Revolutionary: Nature follows precise mathematical relationships discoverable through experiment.

"The book of nature is written in the language of mathematics."
— Galileo Galilei, The Assayer (1623)

Two revolutionary ideas:

  1. Quantitative prediction — Nature follows precise mathematical laws
  2. Experimental method — Truth comes from measurement, not authority

Isaac Newton (1643–1727)

Portrait by Godfrey Kneller, 1689

Principia Mathematica (1687):

$$\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a} = m\frac{d^2\mathbf{x}}{dt^2}$$

This is a differential equation—it relates a quantity to its rates of change.

The Power of Differential Equations

$$\dot{x} = f(x, t)$$

where $\dot{x} = dx/dt$ is the rate of change

Profound implication: If we know the forces and current state, we can predict the future.

This simple-looking equation is the foundation of: classical mechanics, electromagnetism, fluid dynamics, quantum mechanics, population biology, economics...

The Heat Equation (Fourier, 1822)

$$\frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \alpha \frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2}$$
  • Heat spreads smoothly, predictably
  • Can solve analytically with pen and paper
  • No surprises — linear systems are "well-behaved"

Part 3: The Nonlinear World

Where simple models fail

What Linear Models Cannot Capture

  • Turbulent flows
  • Weather systems
  • Ocean currents
  • Living organisms
  • Ecosystems
  • Neural networks
  • Economic markets
  • Climate change
  • Epidemics

Almost everything that matters!

The Navier-Stokes Equations (1845)

$$\rho\left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t} + \mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla\mathbf{v}\right) = -\nabla p + \mu\nabla^2\mathbf{v}$$

The term $\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla\mathbf{v}$ is nonlinear.

  • Cannot solve analytically for turbulent flows
  • Small perturbations can cause dramatic effects
  • Millennium Prize Problem — $1M prize, still unsolved!

Henri Poincaré (1854–1912)

"The last universalist mathematician"

Studied the three-body problem (Sun-Earth-Moon)

  • Newton solved two bodies exactly
  • Add just ONE more body...
  • No general solution exists!

Won King Oscar II of Sweden's prize (1889) — but found something more important than a solution.

Poincaré's Prophetic Discovery

"It may happen that small differences in the initial conditions produce very great ones in the final phenomena. A small error in the former will produce an enormous error in the latter. Prediction becomes impossible."
— Henri Poincaré, Science and Method (1903)

Poincaré discovered chaos theory 60 years before Lorenz — but without computers, he couldn't fully explore it.

The Double Pendulum: Chaos from Simple Rules

Simple mechanics — just two pendulums joined together

Two nearly identical starts:

  • Pendulum A: $\theta = 90°$
  • Pendulum B: $\theta = 90.057°$
  • Difference: 0.001 radians

After a few seconds: completely different trajectories!

Part 4: Computers Change Everything

From 64,000 human computers to ENIAC

The Pre-Computer Limitation

Before ~1950, scientists were limited to:

  • Pen and paper calculations
  • Analytical (closed-form) solutions
  • Human "computers" doing tedious arithmetic
Strong bias towards: linear equations, few variables, simple geometries

Lewis Fry Richardson (1881–1953)

Pioneer of numerical weather prediction

1922: "Weather Prediction by Numerical Process"

  • Attempted 6-hour forecast by hand
  • Calculation took 6 weeks
  • Result: spectacularly wrong!
  • (Numerical instabilities not yet understood)

Richardson's Forecast Factory (1922)

"Imagine a large hall like a theatre... The walls of this chamber are painted to form a map of the globe... A myriad computers are at work upon the weather of the part of the map where each sits..."
— L.F. Richardson, imagining parallel computing
Richardson estimated he would need 64,000 human computers working in perfect synchronization to keep up with real-time weather.

This was essentially parallel computing — envisioned in 1922!

ENIAC (1946)

Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer

  • 18,000 vacuum tubes
  • 1,800 square feet
  • 150 kilowatts of power
  • 5,000 additions per second

The Core Idea: Numerical Integration

# Step forward in time using derivatives
x_new = x_old + dt * f(x_old, t)

This simple idea — Euler's method — unlocked complex systems.

Suddenly: numerical integration of nonlinear differential equations became feasible.

Part 5: The Discovery of Chaos

How a rounding error changed science

Edward Lorenz (1917–2008)

Father of chaos theory

  • MIT meteorologist
  • Running weather simulations on a Royal McBee LGP-30
  • Winter 1961: The famous accident...

The Famous Accident

To repeat a simulation, Lorenz typed in numbers from a printout:

Computer memory:0.506127
Printout:0.506
Difference: 0.0001%

Result: Completely different weather!

The Butterfly Effect

"The initial round-off errors were the culprits; they were steadily amplifying until they dominated the solution."
— Edward Lorenz

Sensitive dependence on initial conditions

"Does the flap of a butterfly's wings in Brazil set off a tornado in Texas?"

The Lorenz System (1963)

Simplified weather model — just 3 equations:

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = \sigma(y - x)$$ $$\frac{dy}{dt} = x(\rho - z) - y$$ $$\frac{dz}{dt} = xy - \beta z$$

Parameters: $\sigma = 10$, $\rho = 28$, $\beta = 8/3$

The Lorenz Attractor

The trajectory never repeats, yet stays bounded — a "strange attractor"

Chaos: What It Means

Determinism ≠ Predictability
  • System follows exact rules (deterministic)
  • Yet long-term prediction is impossible
  • Small errors grow exponentially: $\epsilon(t) \approx \epsilon_0 \cdot e^{\lambda t}$
  • This is why weather forecasts fail after ~10-14 days

Weather Forecast Limits

Better models extend the horizon, but chaos sets fundamental limits

Conway's Game of Life (1970)

Just 4 rules:

  1. Live cell with < 2 neighbors dies (underpopulation)
  2. Live cell with 2-3 neighbors survives
  3. Live cell with > 3 neighbors dies (overpopulation)
  4. Dead cell with exactly 3 neighbors becomes alive
→ Gliders, oscillators, guns, even computers emerge!

The Forward vs. Inverse Problem

Forward: Known equations → Predictions

Equations + Initial Conditions → Simulation → Predictions

Inverse: Data → ???

Observations → ??? → Governing Equations
What if we don't know the equations? Or they're too expensive to solve?

Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

Raissi, Perdikaris, & Karniadakis (2019)

$$\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\mathcal{L}_{\text{data}}}_{\text{fit observations}} + \lambda \underbrace{\mathcal{L}_{\text{physics}}}_{\text{satisfy PDEs}}$$
  • Neural network approximates solution $u_\theta(x,t)$
  • Auto-diff computes $\partial u/\partial t$, $\partial^2 u/\partial x^2$, etc.
  • Penalize PDE violations in the loss function

Recent: PIKANs (Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks), Separable PINNs (100× speedup)

SINDy: Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics

Brunton, Proctor, & Kutz (2016)

$$\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{\Theta}(\mathbf{x}) \boldsymbol{\xi}$$
  • Build library: $\Theta = [1, x, y, z, x^2, xy, \sin(x), \ldots]$
  • Use sparse regression (most $\xi$ are zero)
  • Recover interpretable equations!

Can recover the Lorenz equations from trajectory data alone!
